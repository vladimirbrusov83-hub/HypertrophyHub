My Philosophy

How I think about training — and why most people get it wrong

2 min read
Foundational Framework

Training is simple. Consistent execution is what most athletes fail to deliver.

This knowledge base is built from two decades of coaching competitive bodybuilders, powerlifters, and Special Forces candidates. The population changes. The underlying principles do not. What separates athletes who progress from those who stagnate is rarely a lack of information — it is a failure to execute the fundamentals with sufficient honesty and consistency.

Progressive overload is the singular mechanism that drives long-term adaptation. Every other variable — exercise selection, training frequency, intensity techniques — exists in service of that one objective: accumulating more meaningful work over time. The coaching philosophy here is influenced by High Intensity Training principles, but is not bound to any single system. Application is determined by the athlete, their history, and their goals.

This resource has no tolerance for program-hopping, soreness-chasing, or the addition of complexity before the foundational principles are established. The content presented here reflects how elite athletes are actually coached: directly, with measurable standards, and with a clear distinction between what produces results and what merely produces the appearance of effort.

Entry Level

Beginner Guide

Start here if you're new — everything you actually need to get going

6 min read
⚡ The Whole System in One Rule

"Do 4–8 sets of 6–12 reps every 3–5 days. Add weight when you can. That's it."

Everything else on this page builds on this foundation. Master this before anything else.

Your First Program

Simple Full-Body Program — 3x per week

  • 1–2 exercises per muscle group
  • 2–3 working sets per exercise
  • 8–12 reps per set
  • Rest 1–2 minutes between sets
  • When you complete all sets at the top of your rep range — add weight next session
  • Prioritize consistency over creativity

How to Progress (The Only Rule You Need)

1

Pick a rep range

Start with 8–12 reps. Pick a weight you can do 8 reps with but not 13.

2

Do your sets

Do 3 sets. Stop when you can't do another rep with good form.

3

Track your numbers

Write down your weight and reps every session. This is non-negotiable.

4

Add weight when ready

When you hit 3×12 with good form — add 2.5kg (upper body) or 5kg (lower body) next session.

5

Repeat for months

This alone will build significant muscle. Don't switch programs for at least 3 months.

The 8 Most Common Beginner Mistakes

01
Switching programs too oftenAdaptation takes weeks. Changing before it happens = zero progress. Stick to one program for 3+ months minimum.
02
Never adding weightDoing the same weight forever = maintenance training from day one. Progress requires adding load over time.
03
Chasing sorenessSoreness does NOT equal muscle growth. You can get sore doing useless things. Stop chasing DOMS.
04
Too much volume too soonStarting at 20 sets per muscle per week. 5–8 sets gives better results with lower injury risk for beginners.
05
Not training hard enoughStopping 5+ reps before failure gives almost zero growth stimulus. Your sets need to be challenging.
06
Dropping the weightThe lowering (eccentric) phase is where significant growth happens. Lower the weight slowly — 2–3 seconds down.
07
Skipping warm-upsJumping straight to heavy working sets increases injury risk and reduces performance.
08
Poor exercise choicesIf you can't feel the muscle working, that muscle isn't getting trained. Choose exercises where the target muscle is the weakest link.

Best Exercises for Beginners (Feel-Guaranteed Options)

Quads

Leg Extension — guarantees quad stimulus regardless of your proportions. You don't need to "feel" your quads in squats to train them effectively.

Back

Machine Rows / Cable Rows — removes the low back as the limiting factor. Your back muscles get trained directly, not your lower back.

Chest

Cable Fly / Pec Deck — guarantees chest stimulus. The tricep won't take over like it can in a barbell bench press.

Shoulders

Dumbbell Lateral Raises — go all the way down to your hip on every rep. Full range of motion is critical.

Biceps

Machine Curl / Preacher Curl — keeps arms in position so the shoulder can't take over. Go all the way down for full stretch.

Triceps

Rope Pushdown — simple, effective, easy to feel the tricep working. Go all the way down to full extension.

Legs (Overall)

Leg Press — builds quad, glute, and hamstring mass with less technique demand than a squat. Ideal for beginners who haven't yet developed the mobility or motor pattern for barbell work.

Full Body Strength

Back Squat — the most effective compound movement for lower body development. Trains quads, glutes, hamstrings, and core simultaneously. Learn the pattern early — it pays off for life.

Shoulders & Upper Body

Military Press — overhead pressing builds shoulder strength and stability that carries over to every upper body lift. Start with dumbbells if barbell mobility is an issue.

Theory

Training Principles

The foundational laws that govern how muscle grows

4 min read
Foundation
The SAID Principle

Training must match your goal. If your goal is muscle growth, your training must be specifically designed to cause muscle growth — not just "work hard." Specificity = doing what it takes to cause the desired adaptation.

The Overload Principle

Your body only adapts to stimuli above its current capacity. To keep growing, training must keep exceeding what you're already adapted to. Doing the same workout forever = no progress.

Tension Drives Growth

Mechanical tension from muscles contracting under load is the primary driver of hypertrophy. This is why lowering the weight slowly matters — the eccentric phase generates significant tension.

Deeper
SRA Curve

Every workout causes: Stimulus → Recovery → Adaptation. Train too soon = fatigue without adaptation. Train too late = the adaptation has faded. Train at the right time = compound growth over time.

Soreness ≠ Growth

DOMS (delayed onset muscle soreness) is a separate process from the signals that cause muscle protein synthesis. You can get very sore from things that don't cause growth. Stop chasing soreness.

Strength vs. Size

Bigger muscles are generally stronger, and getting stronger builds bigger muscles — but not 1:1. You can get stronger without getting bigger (neural efficiency) and bigger without proportional strength gains.

Overload and Progressive Overload are NOT the same thing. Overload = any stimulus above baseline. Progressive Overload = systematically increasing the stimulus as you adapt. You can be overloading without progressively overloading.

The FIT Principle — What You Can Manipulate

  • F — Frequency: How often you train a muscle group
  • I — Intensity: How hard you train (load, effort, proximity to failure)
  • T — Time/Volume: Total work done (sets × reps × load)

Deeper

Volume Landmarks (MEV / MAV / MRV)

MEV — Minimum Effective Volume

~5 sets per muscle per week. The minimum to produce a meaningful growth stimulus. Less than this is maintenance or decline.

MAV — Maximum Adaptive Volume

~10–16 sets per muscle per week. The sweet spot where most growth occurs. Going above this rarely produces proportional extra gains.

MRV — Maximum Recoverable Volume

Beyond this point, more volume impairs recovery and causes regression. More is not always better — it can be actively harmful.

Volume When Cutting (Losing Fat)

The biggest mistake people make when cutting is switching to high reps with light weights "to get toned." For natural lifters, this directly causes muscle loss. Maintain intensity — keep the weight on the bar.

Correct approach during a caloric deficit:

  • Reduce volume — you can cut volume and frequency by up to 2/3 and still maintain muscle
  • Maintain intensity — keep the weight on the bar; tension is the signal that tells your body to keep the muscle
  • Less volume + same weight = muscle maintained. The research is clear on this.
Advanced

Set Quality Over Quantity

Not all sets are equal. Warm-up sets do NOT count as working sets. Only challenging sets that bring you close to failure (within 3 reps) provide a true growth stimulus. 10 quality sets beats 20 sloppy sets every time.

Programming

Frequency

How often should you train each muscle group per week?

3 min read
Foundation

🟢 Natural Athletes

  • Optimal: 2x per week per muscle group
  • SRA curve is shorter — you need more frequent stimulation
  • Once-a-week bro splits are suboptimal
  • 3x/week full-body is the historical gold standard
  • Upper/lower split (4 days) hits 2x/week perfectly

🔴 Enhanced Athletes

  • Can grow on 1x/week (bro split)
  • Drugs extend the SRA curve significantly
  • Almost any training produces results on enough drugs
  • Higher injury risk: muscles outgrow connective tissue
  • The rules apply but with wider margins
Drugs widen the parameters in every direction. Enhanced athletes can use lower or higher reps, fewer or more sets, and the lower threshold for load at which training becomes anabolic essentially disappears.
Deeper

High Frequency Warning

Connective Tissue Lags Behind Muscle

Muscle may recover in 48 hours. Tendons and ligaments adapt much more slowly. Training the same muscle 4–6x per week creates disproportionate connective tissue stress — a major reason why daily high-intensity training has high injury rates.

Core Mechanism

Progressive Overload

The engine of muscle growth — how to keep making progress long-term

5 min read
Foundation
Progressive overload is NOT adding weight every session. It is the systematic increase of training stimulus over time as the body adapts. Without progression, training becomes maintenance at best.

The Three Progression Methods

Single Progression
  • Fix the rep count (always do 3×8)
  • Complete all sets → add weight next session
  • Very simple, works well for beginners
  • Limitation: gets harder as weights increase
Double Progression ⭐ Recommended
  • Choose a rep range (e.g., 8–12)
  • Start at the bottom with a given weight
  • Add reps each session until you hit the top
  • Hit the top (3×12)? Add weight and drop back to 8
  • Best system for most people — clear, simple rule
Deeper
Triple Progression
  • Manipulate sets + reps + weight simultaneously
  • Start fewer sets → add sets → add reps → add weight
  • More complex; for advanced athletes or specialization

How Much Weight to Add

  • Upper body: 2–2.5kg (5 lbs) per increment
  • Lower body: 5kg (10 lbs) per increment
  • One extra rep at same weight ≈ adding 2–3% more load. That counts as progress too.
  • As you advance, jumps may need to get smaller (microloading: 0.5–1.25kg plates)

When You Hit a Plateau

1

Don't immediately switch programs

Plateaus are normal. The program is usually not the problem.

2

Continue 3–4 more weeks at the same weight

Stay at the weight that stopped progressing. Your body is still adapting even if reps aren't climbing.

3

Rep out on the last set

On the last session of the 4-week block, do as many reps as possible on the last set.

4

If 2+ extra reps → add weight

If you only got 0–1 extra, continue another 2–4 weeks and retest.

Advanced

Coming Back from Injury

Week-by-week volume rebuild

  1. Week 1: 1 set per exercise, full-body
  2. Week 2: 2 sets per exercise
  3. Week 3: 3 sets per exercise
  4. Week 4: 4 sets per exercise
  5. Week 5: Return to split training

Effort Standards

Intensity & Effort

How hard you need to train — and when going to failure actually matters

4 min read
Foundation

Understanding RIR (Reps in Reserve)

RIR = how many more reps you could do if you had to. This is the most useful measure of training effort.

  • RIR 0 = absolute failure — couldn't do one more rep
  • RIR 1–2 = could do 1–2 more; very hard
  • RIR 3 = the outer edge of effective training for most naturals
  • RIR 4+ = insufficient stimulus; you're essentially just moving weight
If you're doing sets of 20 and stopping at 15, you are not training. The effective reps — the ones that actually cause hypertrophy — are the last 3–5 reps of any set.
Deeper

The Most Important Rule About Failure

Heavy Sets (≤8 reps, 80–85%+ 1RM)

  • Do NOT need to go to failure
  • All motor units recruited from rep 1–2
  • Stimulus is present throughout the set
  • Going to failure on squats/deadlifts = injury risk

Light Sets (15–30 reps)

  • MUST go close to failure
  • Lower-threshold fibers recruited first
  • High-threshold fibers only recruited as fatigue builds
  • Stopping early = wasted set; the growth is at the end

Tempo and Execution

Concentric Phase (Lifting)

Intent to move explosively — even if the weight moves slowly under load. The attempt to accelerate recruits more muscle fibers.

Eccentric Phase (Lowering)

2–3 seconds down, controlled. Never drop the weight. The eccentric is a major driver of hypertrophy and is commonly wasted by rushing.

Bar Speed as Feedback

If your working weight suddenly moves faster than usual — you've adapted. That weight is now too easy. Time to add load. Bar speed tells you when to progress before your reps do.

Advanced

Rep Range Definitions

CategoryRep RangeBest For
Heavy1–5 repsStrength, neural efficiency; still builds muscle with enough volume
Moderate6–12 repsThe hypertrophy sweet spot; best of both worlds
Light13+ repsMetabolic stress, pump; must go close to failure to work

Selection Criteria

Exercise Selection

The right exercise for YOU — why the "best" exercises aren't best for everyone

6 min read
Foundation
Hypertrophy is local. The muscle has to be the limiting factor. If your low back fails on deadlifts before your hamstrings, the deadlift is not training your hamstrings — it's training your low back.

The Core Rule: Target Muscle = Limiting Factor

For any exercise to grow a muscle, that muscle must be the first thing that fatigues. If something else gives out first (low back, triceps, grip), you're training that thing — not your target muscle. Choose exercises where your weak link is the muscle you want to grow.

Deeper

The "Big 5" — When They Work and When They Don't

Barbell Squat

Works great for: People with proportions that let quads be the limiting factor.

Problem for: Tall people with long femurs — low back or upper back typically fails first. Solution: leg extension, hack squat, or leg press.

Barbell Bench Press

Works great for: Short-armed individuals where pecs are the limiting factor.

Problem for: Long-armed people — triceps or anterior delt fails first. Solution: pec deck, cable fly, machine chest press.

Deadlift

Trains "everything" — which in hypertrophy means "nothing specifically." Low back is usually the limiting factor. Better hamstring/glute options: Romanian deadlift, leg curl, hip thrust.

Overhead Press

Front delts are usually already overtrained from bench pressing. Lateral raises and rear delt work may be more productive for physique development.

Bent-Over Row

Low back fatigue limits performance before upper back gets sufficient stimulus. Better: cable rows, machine rows, chest-supported rows — same back muscles, no spinal fatigue.

Advanced

Pre-Exhaustion — Advanced Exercise Order Technique

What it is and when to use it

Do the isolation exercise first, then the compound. The target muscle is pre-fatigued and becomes the limiting factor in the compound — even at lower loads.

Examples: Cable fly → bench press (chest). Leg extension → squat (quads).

Best for: Poor mind-muscle connection, injury accommodation, reducing pressing load to protect joints.

Deloads

How to deload properly

  • Keep the weight on the bar (maintain intensity)
  • Cut volume by up to 2/3 — muscle is fully preserved
  • Reducing volume ≠ losing muscle. Reducing intensity = losing muscle.
  • Strategic full rest of a muscle group can sometimes lead to better development — discipline to hold back is as valuable as discipline to push

Warm-Up Protocol

  • 3–5 warm-up sets before working sets maximum
  • Pyramid up progressively to your working weight
  • Warm-up sets must NOT become working sets — if you're going to failure in warm-ups, you've wasted adaptive capacity before your real training even starts
Advanced

Peak Week / Contest Prep

Pre-Show Training Guidelines
  • Train at 70% of normal working weight
  • Goal: pump and fill the muscle — not strength or progressive overload
  • Reduce resistance training volume significantly
  • No new exercises; no ego lifting; no heroics
  • Final heavy workout: 3–4 days before show
  • Light massage acceptable; deep tissue counterproductive pre-show

Advanced Methods

Special Techniques

Advanced tools to intensify training — use after mastering the basics

4 min read
Deeper
Drop Sets
  • Go to failure (or near), immediately reduce weight 20–30%, continue reps
  • Creates metabolite accumulation beyond standard failure
  • Best on isolation exercises, not complex compounds
  • Use at the end of a set, not the beginning
  • Don't overuse — adds significant fatigue
Supersets (Antagonist Pairs)
  • Alternate opposing muscle groups (biceps + triceps, chest + back)
  • One rests while the other works — increases workout density
  • Maintains pump in both muscle groups simultaneously
  • PAP effect: antagonist relaxation is enhanced while the opposing muscle contracts
Partial Reps
  • Bottom partials: Reps in the stretch position — produces additional stimulus
  • End-of-set partials: After full reps, do 2–3 partials to extend past failure
  • Effective on rows, flies, and curls to squeeze out extra stimulus at the end of a set
Advanced
Rest-Pause / Cluster Sets
  • Do 6–8 reps → rest 20 seconds → 3 reps → rest 20 sec → 2 reps → rest → 1 rep → stop when you're only getting 1
  • Maintains high motor unit recruitment throughout
  • Accumulates significant volume with high-quality reps
  • Less accumulated fatigue than one long set to failure
BFR (Blood Flow Restriction)
  • Cuffs restrict venous outflow — forces metabolite accumulation
  • Recruits high-threshold motor units at 20–30% 1RM
  • Doesn't need to go to failure — metabolites trigger the growth signal
  • Best for post-injury training or adding volume without joint stress

Adaptation

Recovery

Muscle is built between sessions — recovery is half the work

3 min read
Foundation
Sleep

The primary recovery tool. Non-negotiable. No supplement, protocol, or technique compensates for poor sleep. Prioritize it above everything else.

Travel / Jet Lag Recovery

A light workout or low-intensity cardio immediately upon arrival after long flights resets the body clock. Activity — not intensity — is what matters.

Deeper
The Overtraining Problem

Physique competitors commonly come out of shows broken from systematic overtraining — high volume + low calories simultaneously. The correct approach: reduce volume as prep deepens; maintain intensity; replace resistance volume with low-intensity walking.

Injury Prevention Rules

✓
Warm up properly3–5 pyramid warm-up sets. Warm up joints specifically (elbows before arm work). Never go from zero to working weight.
✓
The target muscle must be the limiting factorIf something else hurts or fatigues first, you're injury-loading the wrong structure.
✓
Leg extension: stop short of full lockoutPatellar tendon stress increases exponentially near full extension. The last few degrees are high risk, low reward.
✓
Heavy bench press: respect the loadPec tears are a major risk at heavy loads. Pre-exhaust with flies to reduce pressing load while maintaining stimulus.
✓
Don't add weight too fastConnective tissue adapts much more slowly than muscle. Jumping weight too aggressively causes tendon/ligament failure while the muscle feels fine.
✓
Peak week: no heroicsContest week is not the time to test maxes or try new exercises. 70% intensity, pump only.
CrossFit's random programming combined with technical Olympic lifts performed under fatigue produces disproportionately high injury rates. Specificity and controlled progression exist for a reason.

Nutrition

Calories, macros, fat loss phases, maintenance, and long-term adherence

15 min read
Foundation
The only biological requirement for fat loss is a sustained caloric deficit. All diets that work do so by getting you to eat less. The method doesn't matter — the deficit does. Master tracking first, then manipulate.

Step 1 — Track Before You Change Anything

Establish Your Baseline

Before changing your diet, track your current intake for 1–2 weeks without making any changes. The goal is to find your maintenance calories and your average protein, carb, and fat intake. Every manipulation — deficit, surplus, maintenance — is built from that number.

  • MyFitnessPal — free, large database, best for beginners
  • RP Diet App — auto-plans full diet, adjusts macros meal by meal
  • Do not start a deficit at the same time you start tracking — too much cognitive load, failure rate is high

Macronutrients — What They Are and How to Use Them

Protein

4 cal/gram. Most important macro — preserves muscle, stabilizes blood sugar, maximizes satiety. Keep protein roughly equal across all meals. When creating a deficit, protein is never cut — only carbs and fats are reduced.

Carbohydrates

4 cal/gram. Primary energy source. Place more carbs pre- and post-workout when the body can use them. Lower carb intake at meals far from training (early morning, pre-bed on non-training days).

Fats

9 cal/gram. Digest slowly — do not eat high fat pre- or post-workout. Place fats at meals away from the training window. Minimum floor: 0.3 g per lb of body weight per day. Going below causes hormonal disruption.

Deficit Priority Order
  • 1. Increase physical activity (burns fat, doesn't touch food)
  • 2. Cut fats first
  • 3. Once at fat floor (0.3 g/lb), cut carbs
  • 4. Never cut protein

How to Build a Meal

Every Main Meal Should Include

  • Lean protein — fist-sized portion (chicken, fish, turkey, lean tofu) + 8–12 oz dairy or protein shake
  • Vegetables — fist-sized to 4× fist-sized; grilled, steamed, or raw; avoid soaking in fat
  • Whole grains or fruit — brown rice, oats, whole grain bread, pasta; fresh fruit (not blended or syrup)
  • Healthy fats — nuts, nut butters, avocado, olive oil (in moderation)
Simplification is the key to adherence. Pick 2–4 go-to proteins, veggie combos, carb sources, and fats. Rotate them. Batch cook twice per week and portion into containers. Reduces decision fatigue dramatically.

Fat Loss Phases — Structure

Calculate Your Deficit

Formula: body weight (lbs) × target % loss × 500 = daily calorie deficit.

Example: 150 lbs × 0.005 (0.5%) × 500 = 375 cal/day deficit.

Under 150 lbs: remove ~300 cal/day. Over 250 lbs: up to 700 cal/day.

When Progress Stalls

If for 2+ consecutive weeks you're not hitting your goal rate of loss, remove an additional 250 calories from a combination of fats and carbs. Repeat after another 2 stalled weeks if needed.

Hunger Management
  • More filling per calorie: fresh fruit, raw veggies, Greek yogurt, lean protein
  • Avoid hyper-palatable food at a deficit — amplifies cravings
  • Eat blander food as you get deeper into the diet
  • Time largest meals to your personal hunger peaks
Water Weight & Scale Issues

Normal to retain water while actively losing fat. Causes: high stress, poor sleep, excess salt, artificial drinks.

Fix: standardize salt intake (same shakes per meal), standardize fluids (1-gallon jug daily), cap artificially flavored drinks at a fixed number.

The Maintenance Phase — Why It's Mandatory

After fat loss, the body drives hunger up, craves calorie-dense foods, and reduces spontaneous activity. Most people who succeed at fat loss regain because they skip maintenance. Maintenance phase duration should roughly equal the preceding diet.
1

Don't reintroduce junk immediately

Stick to your diet foods but remove the calorie deficit. Same meals, more of them.

2

Add back ~50% of removed calories

Maintained at 3,000 → dieted at 2,000 → Week 1 of maintenance = 2,500 cal.

3

Expect ~2% immediate weight gain

This is water re-compartmentalization, not fat. It's normal and expected — do not panic and re-diet.

4

Adjust in 250-calorie increments

Dropping below end-of-diet weight → add 250 cal. Climbing above → remove 250 cal. Repeat until stable.

5

Introduce junk only after 3–4 weeks of stability

If weight trends upward for 2+ weeks after junk reintroduction, reduce treats or cut dietary fats slightly.

Advanced

Multi-Phase Fat Loss — Long-Term Structure

Rules for Running Multiple Diet Phases

  • Maximum ~10% body weight lost per single phase
  • Full maintenance phase between each diet — equal length to the diet itself
  • Only start the next phase when: zero diet fatigue, no low energy or cravings, maintenance fully complete
  • Each successive phase will lose less absolute weight — this is correct and expected, not failure
  • Aim to end your last phase 3–4% below your target maintenance weight — life and food reintroduction will push you back up to target

Getting Exotically Lean

Thresholds
  • Males: lean = under 10% BF; exotic = ~5% (veins, visible abs)
  • Females: lean = under 17% BF; exotic = ~12%
  • Not a health requirement — purely aesthetic. Always followed by full maintenance.
Minimum Timeline

Requires at least 2 diet phases: Phase 1 = get lean-lean. Maintenance. Phase 2 = exotic.

Minimum total timeline: 27–36 weeks. Weight training 3–4+x/week mandatory throughout — differentiates muscular lean from flat and thin.

Supplements & Hydration

The Only Supplements Worth It
  • Multivitamin/multimineral — one daily, fills dietary gaps
  • Vitamin D — especially for low sun exposure; gummies work well
  • Fish oil — evidence is mixed; likely harmless, mildly helpful
  • Everything else: no meaningful evidence for the general population
Hydration

Drink one calorie-free beverage with every meal + more when thirsty. Water, sparkling water, diet drinks all count.

Urine check: off-yellow to clear = well hydrated. Dark yellow/brown = drink more.

During fat loss: cap artificially flavored drinks — high volume increases salt cravings and causes water retention.

Long-Term Adherence

Healthy eating becomes habit, not willpower, when structured correctly. The goal is to make the system so automatic it doesn't require decisions: know your go-to foods, your meal schedule, your prep routine. Consistency beats intensity every time.

Practical Adherence Rules

  • Batch cook 2× per week, pre-portion into containers
  • Keep 2–4 go-to options per food category — limit variety during deficit
  • Carry emergency foods: jerky, nuts, protein bars, fresh fruit
  • One bad weekend or vacation doesn't derail progress — return to the plan
  • Check body weight every ~2 weeks; if trending high, tighten up for 2 weeks, then relax
  • If burned out from dieting: stay in healthy balance indefinitely — it's a legitimate outcome

Common Nutrition Mistakes

01
Focusing on food quality instead of quantity Eating "clean" but too many calories. Food quality matters, but a caloric surplus from clean food still causes fat gain.
02
Not tracking before manipulating Estimated portions are systematically underestimated. You can't build a deficit from a number you don't know.
03
Skipping the maintenance phase The #1 reason fat loss is regained. The body's biological drive to regain is real — maintenance is the only countermeasure.
04
Going too aggressive too early Exceeding 1% body weight/week increases muscle loss and diet dropout. Slow phases done correctly beat fast phases abandoned.
05
Trying to cut protein to reduce calories Protein is the last thing to cut. Cutting protein during a deficit accelerates muscle loss and increases hunger.
06
High-fat meals around training Fat slows digestion — pre/post-workout meals should be low fat. High fat pre-workout causes nausea and blunts nutrient delivery.

Advanced Concepts

Deeper science and nuance for experienced trainers

5 min read
Foundation

Natural vs. Enhanced — What Actually Changes

Natural Athletes — Tighter Rules

  • Must train within 2–3 RIR for sets to count
  • Must train each muscle at least 2x/week
  • Cannot get away with pump-only training
  • Progressive overload is the primary driver
  • Every set must be meaningful

Enhanced Athletes — Wider Margins

  • Can grow on 1x/week (extended SRA curve)
  • Almost any training style works on enough drugs
  • Higher injury risk from rapid strength gains
  • Connective tissue can't keep up with muscle growth
  • TRT to normal range ≈ effectively natural for training purposes

Periodization Approaches

Linear Periodization

Add weight or reps every session or every few sessions. Works until plateaus become frequent. Ideal for beginners — simple and effective.

Block Periodization

Focus on one quality (strength, volume, intensity) for a training block (4–8 weeks) then shift emphasis. Allows specialization without overreaching.

Advanced
Daily Undulating Periodization (DUP)

Vary intensity and volume within the same week. Monday: heavy 5s. Wednesday: moderate 8s. Friday: high-rep 12–15s. Not for beginners — too much complexity before foundations are solid.

HIT (High Intensity Training) — History & Reality

The HIT lineage

  • Arthur Jones (1970s): Nautilus machines + ~9 true limit sets/week. Rational and reasonable.
  • Mike Mentzer: Extended Jones to extremes — eventually recommending one maximal effort every two weeks. Effective only if actually executed to true failure (rare).
  • Dorian Yates: One top set to failure + multiple warm-up sets. Not one total set. Elite intensity, significant injuries.

The critical rule: HIT and high volume are mutually exclusive. Pick one system and follow it completely:

  • HIT: 2–3 working sets/muscle, absolute failure, 5+ days between sessions
  • Volume: 15–20 sets/muscle, stop 2–3 reps short of failure, frequent sessions

Myofibrillar vs. Sarcoplasmic Hypertrophy

Myofibrillar Hypertrophy

Growth of contractile proteins (actin and myosin). Associated with heavier training (lower reps). Makes muscles stronger and denser.

Sarcoplasmic Hypertrophy

Growth of fluid and energy storage components of the muscle cell. Associated with higher-rep, pump-focused training. Contributes to muscle size visually.

Psychology

Mindset & Application

The psychological determinants of long-term training success

3 min read
The repetitions performed past the initial discomfort threshold — where most athletes terminate a set — are the repetitions that produce the majority of the adaptive response. The ability to distinguish genuine muscular failure from discomfort is a trainable skill and a critical determinant of long-term progress.
Foundation
Execution Consistency Outperforms Program Sophistication

A well-structured program executed with full adherence over months produces superior outcomes to a theoretically optimal program executed inconsistently. Program quality is not the limiting variable for the majority of athletes — consistent, honest execution is.

The Actual Definition of Training to Failure

The majority of athletes who believe they train to failure do not. They terminate sets at the threshold of discomfort, not at true muscular failure. The capacity to train genuinely to failure is a skill that develops with training age and, frequently, under the supervision of an experienced coach.

Individualisation of Optimal Parameters

Optimal training parameters are athlete-specific, goal-specific, and time-specific — they evolve as the athlete develops. There is no universal optimal; there are principles, and then there is the individual application of those principles. Systematic execution matters more than theoretical perfection.

Deeper
Psychological Compatibility in Program Selection

Athletes respond differently to different training philosophies — not exclusively for physiological reasons, but due to psychological fit. A program the athlete will not execute consistently is not optimal regardless of its theoretical merit. Sustainable adherence is a genuine performance variable.

Social Accountability and Adherence

Training partners and structured accountability systems consistently improve session attendance and effort levels. Long-term training consistency — the most significant determinant of overall results — is substantially enhanced by structured social accountability.

Advanced
Strategic Deloading as a Development Tool

Deliberate reduction or temporary cessation of training for a specific muscle group can produce superior subsequent development. The discipline to reduce training at the appropriate time is as important to long-term progress as the discipline to train with intensity.

Universal Agreements

  • Full range of motion is essential — don't cut the movement short
  • Warm-up sets must not become working sets
  • Training intensity must be sufficient for growth — not going through the motions
  • Progressive loading is the goal over time
  • Volume must be managed relative to recovery capacity
  • The target muscle must receive the stimulus — mind-muscle connection matters
